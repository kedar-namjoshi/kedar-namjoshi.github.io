Research Highlights

Developing correct and secure programs is challenging. My research is on methods that simplify the construction of programs, and offer mathematical guarantees of their correctness and security.

Much of the research is driven by the fascinating interplay between deductive and algorithmic approaches to program verification. A deductive proof method can suggest new automated approaches: e.g., using fixpoint calculations to produce compositional assume-guarantee proofs. And, on the other hand, algorithmic verification methods can be viewed as proof generators: e.g., a model checker can produce independently-checkable proof certificates to justify why a property holds of the program.

My research includes work on

  • Parameterized verification: Cutoff theorems which show that one can establish correctness of protocols for arbitrarily large network sizes by analyzing a few small instances, up to the cutoff bound
  • Program equivalence: A well-founded bisimulation proof method that considerably simplifies proofs of program equivalence, e.g., for pipeline processors and compiler optimizations
  • Abstraction principles: Linkages between deductive proofs, model checking and abstraction, and their use for fast, incremental program verification
  • Compositional reasoning: Fixpoint methods for compositional (i.e., divide-and-conquer) analysis of large concurrent programs, and neighborhood symmetry principles that simplify such analysis and provide broadly applicable cutoff theorems
  • Self-certification: Self-certification of model checkers, compiler optimizations and network route calculations, using generated proof witnesses
This research has been supported, in part, by grants from the NSF and DARPA.
